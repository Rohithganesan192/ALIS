# Step 3 – Dataset Engineering

## 3.1 Introduction

The knowledge base is the foundation of the Assembly Line Information System (ALIS). Unlike generative conversational systems that produce responses using pretrained language models or external knowledge sources, ALIS is designed as a closed-domain, retrieval-based chatbot. Every response generated by the system originates from a developer-authored dataset created from direct internship experience within an automobile assembly shop. Consequently, the quality, organization, and completeness of the dataset directly determine the overall performance, reliability, and usefulness of the chatbot.

Following the completion of the System Analysis phase (Step 1) and the System Design phase (Step 2), the project now enters Dataset Engineering. This phase focuses entirely on designing the knowledge base before any implementation begins. Rather than immediately creating dataset records, this phase establishes the engineering standards that every record must follow. By separating dataset design from dataset construction, the project minimizes inconsistencies, improves maintainability, simplifies future expansion, and ensures that every record follows a uniform structure.

Dataset Engineering defines the complete blueprint for the ALIS knowledge base. It specifies the dataset requirements, logical organization, physical storage structure, category hierarchy, and record standards that govern the construction of the dataset. Once these design decisions are finalized, the subsequent implementation phase can focus exclusively on populating the dataset without revisiting structural decisions.

The dataset designed during this phase is derived exclusively from the developer's fifteen-day internship in an automobile assembly shop. Every piece of knowledge included in the chatbot originates from direct observation or information communicated during the internship. This deliberate design choice ensures that ALIS remains a domain-specific, data-grounded information system whose responses are fully traceable to developer-authored knowledge rather than external or generated content.

# Task 3.1 – Dataset Requirements Design

## 3.1.1 Purpose of the Dataset

The dataset serves as the single source of domain knowledge for the Assembly Line Information System. Since ALIS follows a retrieval-based architecture, it does not generate new information or retrieve information from external systems. Instead, it searches within its predefined knowledge base and returns the most appropriate answer available. Consequently, the dataset defines the complete knowledge boundary of the chatbot.

Every question that ALIS can answer must already exist, directly or indirectly, within the dataset. Likewise, any information absent from the dataset is beyond the chatbot's capabilities. Therefore, the dataset is not merely a supporting component but the primary determinant of the chatbot's usefulness and correctness.

The purpose of this task is to establish the requirements that the dataset must satisfy before implementation begins. These requirements ensure that the dataset remains accurate, consistent, maintainable, traceable, and suitable for supporting reliable information retrieval.

---

## 3.1.2 Importance of the Dataset in a Retrieval-Based NLP Chatbot

In generative conversational systems, the knowledge source is only one contributor to the quality of responses. In contrast, ALIS is a closed-domain retrieval-based chatbot where the dataset completely defines the system's knowledge.

This creates two important consequences.

First, the dataset establishes the maximum capability of the chatbot. Even the most sophisticated retrieval algorithm cannot answer questions whose knowledge does not exist within the dataset.

Second, errors in chatbot responses are more likely to originate from incomplete, inconsistent, or inaccurate dataset entries than from the retrieval mechanism itself. Since retrieval follows deterministic mathematical procedures, improving the dataset has a greater impact on chatbot quality than increasing algorithmic complexity.

For this reason, dataset engineering represents one of the most important engineering activities within the entire ALIS project.

---

## 3.1.3 Data Sources

The ALIS dataset is constructed exclusively from the developer's internship experience conducted within an automobile assembly shop. The knowledge base consists of observations, explanations, procedures, equipment descriptions, safety practices, workflow sequences, and personnel responsibilities encountered during the internship.

The following sources contribute to the dataset:

- Direct observation of assembly-line activities.
- Internship notes prepared during the placement.
- Explanations provided by engineers, supervisors, and operators.
- Station-level workflow observations.
- Safety demonstrations and operational practices.

To preserve data authenticity and traceability, the following sources are explicitly excluded:

- Public manufacturing manuals.
- Automotive websites.
- AI-generated content.
- Web-scraped information.
- General manufacturing knowledge outside the internship experience.
- Information obtained from external APIs or online services.

This single-source strategy ensures that every dataset record can be traced back to a genuine internship observation.

The ALIS dataset has the following objectives:

1. Represent the most common questions likely to be asked by first-time assembly shop visitors.
2. Provide one clear and authoritative answer for every knowledge record.

4. Support reliable information retrieval through consistent record organization.
5. Remain completely developer-authored and manually verifiable.
6. Support future dataset expansion without structural redesign.
7. Maintain complete traceability between chatbot responses and original internship knowledge.
8. Serve as the single source of truth for the chatbot.

# Task 3.2 – Dataset Schema Design

## 3.2.1 Purpose of the Schema

Task 3.1 established the functional and quality requirements of the ALIS dataset. The next logical step is to define how every knowledge record should be organized. The dataset schema provides this structural definition by specifying the logical organization of every record independent of its physical storage format.

A schema serves as the structural contract between the dataset and every future component that will interact with it. Rather than defining implementation details such as CSV columns or JSON properties, the schema specifies the logical fields every record must contain, the purpose of each field, and the relationships between records. Establishing this structure before dataset construction ensures that all records remain consistent throughout the project lifecycle.

---

## 3.2.2 Design Principles

The schema is governed by the following principles.

### Consistency

Every knowledge record shall follow exactly the same logical structure. Uniformity allows both human reviewers and future software components to interpret every record without requiring special handling.

### Simplicity

The schema contains only the fields necessary to satisfy the project's functional requirements. Unnecessary fields increase authoring complexity without providing measurable benefits.

### Extensibility

The schema shall support future expansion of both record count and category count without structural redesign.

### Maintainability

Every field shall have one clearly defined responsibility. Overloaded fields are intentionally avoided to simplify future maintenance.

### Traceability

Every record shall remain uniquely identifiable so that any chatbot response can be traced back to its originating knowledge record.

### Conceptual Normalization

Each distinct piece of knowledge shall appear only once within the dataset. Multiple phrasings of the same question shall belong to one record rather than multiple duplicated records.

---

## 3.2.3 Overall Dataset Organization

The ALIS dataset is organized as a flat collection of independent records.

Unlike hierarchical document structures or relational database models, every knowledge record exists independently and does not reference any other record. This organization directly supports the retrieval-based architecture established during Step 2, where each record represents an individual retrieval unit.

Categories function as descriptive metadata rather than structural containers. Records belong to categories, but categories do not contain nested records.

---

## 3.2.4 Record-Based Design

The fundamental unit of the ALIS knowledge base is the knowledge record.

Each record represents one independently retrievable unit of information consisting of a single question-answer relationship.

This design offers several advantages:

- Independent retrieval.
- Clear traceability.
- Simplified maintenance.
- Straightforward evaluation.
- Easy future expansion.

Every record remains self-contained, eliminating dependencies between records.

---

## 3.2.5 Required Fields

Every knowledge record contains the following mandatory logical fields.

### Identifier

A unique identifier that distinguishes the record from every other record throughout its lifetime.

### Category

The knowledge category to which the record belongs.

### Primary Question

The canonical question representing the knowledge contained within the record.

### Answer

The authoritative response associated with the primary question.

### Source Context

A short internal description identifying where the knowledge originated during the internship.

Together, these fields provide the minimum information necessary for retrieval, maintenance, validation, and traceability.

---

## 3.2.6 Optional Fields

The schema also supports several optional fields.

### Alternate Questions

Additional natural-language phrasings representing the same underlying question.

### Terminology Notes

Clarifications for abbreviations or technical terminology appearing within the record.

### Confidence Note

An internal indication of the developer's confidence regarding the record's accuracy.

Optional fields enhance usability without altering the core structure of the dataset.

---

## 3.2.7 Unique Identifier Strategy

Every knowledge record shall possess one unique identifier satisfying the following properties:

- Globally unique.
- Stable throughout the record lifecycle.
- Independent of physical ordering.
- Human-readable where practical.

The identifier enables complete traceability between chatbot responses and dataset records.

Identifiers remain unchanged even if the content of the record is modified.

---

## 3.2.8 Category Organization

Categories provide logical grouping rather than structural organization.

The finalized category set forms a closed vocabulary established before dataset construction begins.

Every record belongs to exactly one category.

This approach prevents:

- Category drift.
- Duplicate classification.
- Ambiguous record ownership.

Categories support dataset navigation, evaluation, and reporting while maintaining structural simplicity.

---

## 3.2.9 Relationship Between Records

Knowledge records remain completely independent.

No record references another record.

No parent-child hierarchy exists.

No conversational relationships exist.

This design directly supports the single-turn interaction model established during Step 2.

The only relationship shared between records is membership within the same category.

---

## 3.2.10 Handling Duplicate Knowledge

Duplicate knowledge represents a schema violation.

Whenever two questions correspond to the same underlying knowledge, they shall be represented by one record containing:

- One authoritative answer.
- One primary question.
- Multiple alternate question phrasings where necessary.

This approach prevents inconsistencies that may arise when duplicated knowledge evolves independently.

---

## 3.2.11 Handling Multiple Question Phrasings

Users naturally ask identical questions using different wording.

Rather than creating separate records for each phrasing, ALIS associates multiple question variations with a single knowledge record.

Each record therefore contains:

- One canonical question.
- Zero or more alternate phrasings.
- One authoritative answer.

This organization preserves both retrieval flexibility and dataset consistency.

---

## 3.2.12 Handling Terminology

Assembly shop terminology frequently includes abbreviations and technical vocabulary.

To maintain consistency:

- One preferred term shall be used throughout answer content.
- Alternative terminology shall be documented using terminology notes where necessary.
- Technical explanations shall remain concise and consistent.

Terminology handling is therefore standardized rather than improvised during record creation.

---

## 3.2.13 Future Expansion

The schema supports future expansion through:

- Additional records.
- Additional categories.
- Additional alternate question phrasings.

These additions require no structural redesign because the schema remains independent of dataset size.

However, the current schema intentionally excludes:

- Multiple answers per record.
- Inter-record references.
- Conversational context.
- Multi-turn dialogue history.

These capabilities remain outside the scope of the current project.

---

## 3.2.14 Schema Validation Requirements

Before a knowledge record becomes part of the dataset, it shall satisfy the following validation requirements:

- Every mandatory field is present.
- The identifier is unique.
- The category belongs to the finalized category list.
- The record represents exactly one knowledge unit.
- Alternate questions correspond to the same underlying knowledge.
- No duplicate knowledge already exists elsewhere within the dataset.

These validation requirements preserve consistency throughout dataset construction.

---

## 3.2.15 Expected Outcome

Completion of the Dataset Schema Design establishes the logical blueprint governing every knowledge record within ALIS.

Specifically, the schema defines:

- Record organization.
- Mandatory fields.
- Optional fields.
- Identifier strategy.
- Category organization.
- Record independence.
- Duplicate handling.
- Terminology handling.
- Future expansion capability.
- Validation requirements.

This logical schema serves as the direct foundation for the Physical Dataset Design developed in Task 3.3.

# Task 3.3 – Physical Dataset Design

## 3.3.1 Purpose of the Physical Dataset Design

Task 3.2 established the logical organization of every knowledge record. This task translates that logical design into a concrete physical representation by defining the files, fields, naming conventions, and storage organization that will be used during dataset construction.

The physical dataset design does not change any logical decisions established previously. Instead, it provides a standardized implementation blueprint that ensures every knowledge record is stored consistently, remains human-readable, and supports future maintenance.

By separating logical and physical design, ALIS allows structural improvements to storage without affecting the underlying knowledge model.

---

## 3.3.2 Why a Structured Dataset is Required

The ALIS knowledge base is designed as a structured dataset rather than a collection of free-form notes or documents.

A structured dataset provides several engineering advantages:

- Every knowledge record follows the same format.
- Records can be reviewed consistently.
- Information remains easy to maintain.
- Traceability is preserved.
- Future processing becomes straightforward.
- Human errors during authoring are reduced.

Since the chatbot retrieves individual records rather than paragraphs of documentation, structured storage is essential.

---

## 3.3.3 Dataset File Organization

The dataset is organized into separate files based on responsibility.

The physical organization consists of:

- One primary dataset file containing all chatbot knowledge.
- One evaluation dataset containing testing queries.
- One optional category reference file defining the finalized categories.

All dataset files are stored within the project's `data/` directory established during Step 2.

Separating these files prevents mixing operational knowledge with evaluation material while improving maintainability.

---

## 3.3.4 Primary Dataset File

The primary dataset file serves as the single authoritative knowledge base for ALIS.

It contains every approved knowledge record that the chatbot may retrieve during operation.

The primary dataset possesses the following characteristics:

- One authoritative knowledge source.
- Approximately 80–120 records.
- Flat record organization.
- Human-readable storage.
- Manual authoring.
- Version-controlled updates.

Knowledge is not distributed across multiple primary files because doing so would increase maintenance complexity and risk inconsistencies.

---

## 3.3.5 Supporting Dataset Files

Supporting files complement the primary dataset without becoming part of the chatbot's operational knowledge base.

### Evaluation Dataset

This file contains manually created evaluation queries together with their expected answers.

Its purpose is to measure retrieval quality independently from the operational dataset.

### Category Reference

This optional file lists:

- Finalized category names.
- Category codes.
- Category descriptions.

Maintaining category definitions separately reduces the likelihood of inconsistent category usage during dataset construction.

---

## 3.3.6 Physical Record Layout

Every knowledge record occupies exactly one physical record within the dataset.

Each record contains the same fields arranged in the same order throughout the dataset.

No record spans multiple rows.

No row represents multiple knowledge records.

This one-to-one correspondence simplifies:

- Maintenance.
- Validation.
- Traceability.
- Future processing.

Consistent ordering also improves manual readability.

---

## 3.3.7 Physical Field Definitions

The physical dataset contains the following fields.

| Field            | Purpose                             | Mandatory | Data Type |
| ---------------- | ----------------------------------- | --------- | --------- |
| record_id        | Unique record identifier            | Yes       | String    |
| category         | Knowledge category                  | Yes       | String    |

| answer           | Authoritative response              | Yes       | String    |
| source_note      | Internship knowledge origin         | Yes       | String    |
| question_alt     | Alternate question phrasings        | No        | String    |
| terminology_note | Technical terminology clarification | No        | String    |

These fields represent the physical implementation of the logical schema defined during Task 3.2.

---

## 3.3.8 Data Types

Each physical field uses an appropriate textual representation.

### Record Identifier

Structured alphanumeric string.

### Category

Controlled vocabulary string.

### Question

Natural-language text.

### Answer

Natural-language text.

### Source Note

Short descriptive text.

### Alternate Questions

Delimited text containing multiple question variations.

### Terminology Note

Short explanatory text.

### Confidence Note

Controlled textual value such as:

- High
- Medium

---

## 3.3.9 Mandatory and Optional Fields

The physical dataset distinguishes between mandatory and optional information.

### Mandatory Fields

- record_id
- category
- question_primary
- answer
- source_note

These fields must be present in every record.

### Optional Fields

- question_alt
- terminology_note
- confidence_note

Optional fields remain available whenever additional information improves documentation quality but are not required for every knowledge record.

---

## 3.3.10 Naming Conventions

To maintain consistency throughout the dataset, standardized naming conventions are adopted for files, fields, categories, and identifiers.

### Field Naming

All field names use lowercase letters with words separated by underscores.

Examples include:

- `record_id`
- `category`
- `question_primary`
- `answer`
- `source_note`

This convention improves readability while remaining compatible with common programming and data-processing tools.

### Category Naming

Category values use standardized names defined during Task 3.4. Every category name is written consistently throughout the dataset to avoid accidental duplication caused by spelling or capitalization differences.

### File Naming

Dataset files follow descriptive, lowercase names separated by underscores. File names clearly indicate their purpose and remain independent of implementation details.

Consistent naming supports maintainability, readability, and future automation.

---

## 3.3.11 Record Numbering Strategy

Every knowledge record receives a unique identifier consisting of a category code followed by a sequential number.

This strategy provides several advantages:

- Globally unique identifiers.
- Human-readable record references.
- Easy manual navigation.
- Stable traceability.
- Independent numbering within each category.

Record identifiers remain unchanged throughout the lifecycle of the record, even if its contents are modified.

Sequential numbers are never reused after record retirement, preserving historical traceability.

---

## 3.3.12 Category Coding Strategy

Each finalized category is assigned a fixed abbreviation used within record identifiers.

The coding strategy satisfies the following requirements:

- Short and easily recognizable.
- Unique across the dataset.
- Stable throughout the project.
- Clearly associated with one category.

Category codes support compact identifiers while maintaining human readability.

The mapping between category names and category codes is maintained in the category reference file.

---

## 3.3.13 File Naming Standards

Every dataset file follows a consistent naming convention.

The naming standard includes:

- Lowercase characters only.
- Words separated using underscores.
- Descriptive file names.
- Appropriate file extensions.

This convention makes file purposes immediately recognizable while avoiding operating-system-specific naming issues.

Examples include:

- Primary dataset
- Evaluation dataset
- Category reference

The exact filenames remain consistent throughout the project.

## 3.3.14 Dataset Versioning Strategy

The dataset evolves incrementally throughout development.

Version management is achieved using the project's Git repository rather than embedding version information within individual records.

The versioning strategy provides:

- Complete history of dataset changes.
- Safe rollback to previous versions.
- Transparent documentation of modifications.

Significant milestones may additionally be documented within project documentation, while individual record histories remain available through version control.

Such changes require careful review to preserve consistency and traceability.

## 3.3.17 Dataset Consistency Rules

In addition to structural integrity, the dataset must maintain content consistency.

- Duplicate knowledge is prohibited.

Upon completion of dataset construction, the physical knowledge base will consist of:

# 3.4 Dataset Category Design

## Overview

The Dataset Category Design defines the complete classification structure used throughout the ALIS knowledge base. Every dataset record belongs to exactly one category, ensuring consistent organization, easier maintenance, improved traceability, and balanced knowledge coverage.

This design builds directly upon the Dataset Schema Design and Physical Dataset Design by finalizing the category hierarchy, category boundaries, category codes, and category assignment rules before dataset construction begins.

---

## 3.4.1 Purpose of Category Design

Category design provides a standardized method for organizing assembly shop knowledge into clearly distinguishable knowledge areas.

The objectives are to:

- Organize the knowledge base logically.
- Improve maintainability.
- Support category-wise evaluation.
- Prevent category overlap.
- Maintain consistency during dataset authoring.
- Provide category codes for record identifiers.

---

## 3.4.2 Need for Categorization

A retrieval-based chatbot operates on discrete knowledge records.

Categorization allows:

- Easier dataset navigation.
- Balanced knowledge distribution.
- Structured dataset growth.
- Better record management.
- Simplified dataset validation.
- Category-wise performance evaluation.

Without predefined categories, knowledge organization becomes inconsistent and difficult to maintain.

---

## 3.4.3 Category Design Principles

The category structure follows five major principles.

### Groundedness

Every category must originate from actual knowledge observed during the internship.

No speculative category is introduced.

### Mutual Distinctiveness

Each category should represent a unique area of knowledge with minimal conceptual overlap.

### Exhaustiveness

Together, all categories should cover the complete intended knowledge scope of ALIS.

### Stability

Category definitions remain fixed after dataset authoring begins.

### Simplicity

The number of categories remains small enough for consistent manual maintenance.

---

## 3.4.4 Final Category Structure

The ALIS knowledge base contains six finalized categories.

| Category                   | Purpose                                      |
| -------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| Safety                     | Safety procedures and protective measures    |
| Assembly Procedures        | Station workflows and operating procedures   |
| Equipment and Tools        | Machines, tools and equipment                |
| Roles and Responsibilities | Personnel duties and responsibilities        |
| General Orientation        | Introductory shop-floor knowledge            |
| Quality and Performance    | Quality inspection and performance practices |

This category set represents the complete knowledge scope for the current project.

---

## 3.4.5 Category Descriptions

### Safety

Contains knowledge regarding:

- Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)
- Hazard awareness
- Emergency procedures
- Safety rules
- Safe working practices

Safety information is treated as a separate category because it is critical across every assembly activity.

---

### Assembly Procedures

Contains knowledge describing:

- Assembly workflows
- Station operations
- Process sequences
- Standard operating procedures
- Manufacturing activities

This represents the core operational knowledge of the assembly shop.

---

### Equipment and Tools

Contains knowledge regarding:

- Machine names
- Tool names
- Equipment purpose
- Equipment usage
- Functional descriptions

The focus is on identifying equipment rather than describing operational procedures.

---

### Roles and Responsibilities

Contains information about:

- Operators
- Supervisors
- Quality inspectors
- Technicians
- Other personnel

The emphasis is on responsibilities rather than operational workflow.

---

### General Orientation

Contains introductory information such as:

- Basic terminology
- Shop-floor concepts
- Visitor information
- Manufacturing vocabulary
- General assembly shop understanding

This category helps first-time users understand the assembly environment.

---

### Quality and Performance

Contains information regarding:

- Quality inspections
- Quality standards
- Performance expectations
- Inspection practices
- Quality assurance activities

Only knowledge directly observed during the internship is included.

---

## 3.4.6 Category Scope

Each category has clearly defined boundaries.

| Category                   | Included                           | Excluded               |
| -------------------------- | ---------------------------------- | ---------------------- |
| Safety                     | PPE, hazards, emergency procedures | General procedures     |
| Assembly Procedures        | Workflow and process steps         | Equipment descriptions |
| Equipment and Tools        | Machines and tools                 | Workflow sequences     |
| Roles and Responsibilities | Personnel duties                   | Detailed procedures    |
| General Orientation        | Basic concepts                     | Specialized knowledge  |
| Quality and Performance    | Inspection and quality             | Equipment operation    |

These boundaries reduce ambiguity during dataset authoring.

---

## 3.4.7 Category Boundaries

Category assignment follows one fundamental rule:

> Every record is classified according to its **primary intent**.

If a record references multiple topics, it is assigned to the category representing its main purpose.

This prevents duplicate classification and maintains a simple dataset structure.

---

## 3.4.8 Handling Overlapping Knowledge

Some assembly shop knowledge naturally spans multiple topics.

To maintain consistency, ALIS follows three rules.

### Primary Intent Rule

Records are categorized based on the main knowledge they communicate.

### Terminology Reference Rule

Related concepts may be referenced within answers or terminology notes without duplicating complete information.

### Precedence Rule

When classification remains uncertain, categories are prioritized in the following order:

1. Safety
2. Assembly Procedures

# 3.5 Dataset Record Design

## Overview

The Dataset Record Design defines the structure, lifecycle, authoring standards, validation process, and quality expectations for an individual knowledge record within the ALIS dataset. Since the record is the smallest retrievable unit of knowledge, maintaining consistency across every record is essential for reliable retrieval, traceability, and long-term maintainability.

This document builds upon the Dataset Schema Design, Physical Dataset Design, and Dataset Category Design by defining how every individual record is created, reviewed, approved, modified, and maintained.

---

## 3.5.1 Purpose of Dataset Record Design

The purpose of record design is to establish a standardized method for creating every knowledge entry within the ALIS dataset.

It ensures that:

- Every record follows an identical structure.
- Knowledge remains self-contained.
- Records are easy to review and maintain.
- Duplicate knowledge is prevented.
- Each record can be independently retrieved.
- Dataset quality remains consistent throughout development.

---

## 3.5.2 Definition of a Knowledge Record

A knowledge record is the smallest independent unit of information stored in the ALIS dataset.

Each record represents:

- One question
- One authoritative answer
- One category
- One unique identifier
- One source reference

A record is completely self-contained and does not depend on any other record for interpretation.

---

## 3.5.3 Record Design Principles

Every dataset record follows five primary principles.

### Atomicity

A record must represent exactly one piece of knowledge.

Multiple unrelated questions must never be combined into one record.

---

### Self-Sufficiency

A record must be understandable without requiring any additional context.

Users should receive a complete answer from a single record.

---

### Fidelity

Every record must accurately represent information observed or communicated during the internship.

No assumptions or external information may be added.

---

### Economy

Only information necessary to answer the question should be included.

Unnecessary explanations should be avoided.

---

### Reviewability

Each record should be simple enough for manual review by the developer or an evaluator.

---

## 3.5.4 Characteristics of a Good Record

A high-quality record should satisfy the following characteristics:

- Natural question wording.
- Clear and complete answer.
- Correct category assignment.
- No duplicate knowledge.
- Specific source note.
- Consistent terminology.
- Independent readability.
- Accurate representation of internship knowledge.

---

## 3.5.5 Record Lifecycle

Each record progresses through five lifecycle stages.

### Stage 1 – Draft

The record has been created but not yet reviewed.

---

### Stage 2 – Reviewed

The record has been checked for correctness and completeness.

---

### Stage 3 – Approved

The record becomes part of the official dataset.

---

### Stage 4 – Modified

Changes have been made after approval.

The record must undergo review again.

---

### Stage 5 – Retired

The record is removed from active use while preserving its identifier for traceability.

---

## 3.5.6 Record Creation Process

Creating a new record follows a structured workflow.

### Step 1

Identify a unique piece of internship knowledge.

### Step 2

Determine the correct category.

### Step 3

Reserve a new unique record identifier.

### Step 4

Write the question, answer, and source note.

### Step 5

Check for duplicate knowledge before adding the record.

Only after completing these steps does the record enter the Draft stage.

---

## 3.5.7 Record Review Process

Every draft record undergoes a formal review before approval.

The review verifies:

- Mandatory field completeness.
- Correct category assignment.
- Writing quality.
- Terminology consistency.
- Duplicate prevention.
- Overall record quality.

If any issue is identified, the record returns to the Draft stage for correction.

---

## 3.5.8 Record Approval Process

A record becomes part of the official dataset only after approval.

Approval requires:

- Successful review.
- Validation of all mandatory fields.
- Unique identifier confirmation.
- Successful duplicate check.
- Compliance with dataset standards.

Only approved records are considered authoritative knowledge.

---

## 3.5.9 Record Modification Policy

Approved records may be modified if improvements or corrections are necessary.

The following rules apply:

- Record identifiers remain unchanged.
- Modified records return to the review stage.
- Category changes require exceptional review.
- Every modification must preserve dataset consistency.

---

## 3.5.10 Record Retirement Policy

A record may be retired when:

- It is discovered to be incorrect.
- Duplicate knowledge exists.
- It no longer belongs within the dataset scope.

Retired records:

- Are removed from active use.
- Keep their original identifiers.
- Include a documented retirement reason.

Identifiers are never reused.

---

## 3.5.11 Writing Guidelines for Questions

Questions should satisfy the following guidelines:

- Written naturally.
- Easy for first-time visitors to understand.
- Focus on one topic only.
- Avoid technical jargon unless necessary.
- Represent realistic user queries.
- Alternate phrasings should express the same intent.

---

## 3.5.12 Writing Guidelines for Answers

Answers should:

- Fully answer the question.
- Use simple language.
- Remain factually accurate.
- Contain only internship-based knowledge.
- Maintain consistent terminology.
- Avoid unnecessary detail.

Each answer should remain independent of every other record.

---

## 3.5.13 Writing Guidelines for Source Notes

Source notes provide internal traceability.

They should:

- Identify where the information originated.
- Be concise.
- Reference stations, observations, or discussions.
- Avoid introducing additional knowledge.
- Support future review.

Source notes are not intended for chatbot responses.

---

## 3.5.14 Common Mistakes to Avoid

The following issues should be avoided during dataset construction.

- Compound questions.
- Duplicate knowledge.
- Incorrect category assignment.
- Unsupported assumptions.
- Inconsistent terminology.
- Missing mandatory fields.
- Skipping the review process.

Avoiding these mistakes improves overall dataset quality.

---

## 3.5.15 Record Quality Checklist

Before approval, every record should satisfy the following checklist.

- Answers one question only.
- Uses natural language.
- Provides a complete answer.
- Assigned to the correct category.
- Includes a valid source note.
- Contains no duplicate knowledge.
- Uses consistent terminology.
- Mandatory fields are complete.
- Identifier follows project standards.

Only records satisfying every checklist item should be approved.

---

## 3.5.16 Expected Outcome

Completion of the Dataset Record Design establishes a standardized process for creating and maintaining every ALIS knowledge record.

This design ensures:

- Uniform record structure.
- Consistent writing standards.
- Reliable review procedures.
- Controlled record lifecycle.
- High-quality dataset construction.
- Long-term maintainability.
- Complete traceability.

With this document completed, all remaining work shifts from dataset design to dataset population and validation.

# 3.6 Dataset Validation Design

This document builds upon the Dataset Requirements, Schema Design, Physical Dataset Design, Category Design, and Record Design by defining how dataset quality is verified before implementation begins.

---

## 3.6.1 Purpose of Dataset Validation

The purpose of dataset validation is to ensure that every knowledge record included in ALIS satisfies all predefined design requirements before it becomes part of the official knowledge base.

Dataset validation aims to:

- Maintain dataset integrity.
- Prevent incomplete records.
- Detect duplicate knowledge.
- Ensure consistent terminology.
- Verify category correctness.
- Support reliable chatbot responses.

---

## 3.6.2 Objectives of Validation

The validation process has the following objectives:

- Verify structural correctness.
- Verify logical correctness.
- Verify record completeness.
- Verify traceability.
- Maintain category consistency.
- Ensure overall dataset quality.

Validation is performed before records are considered finalized.

---

## 3.6.3 Validation Principles

The ALIS dataset validation process follows five principles.

### Completeness

Every mandatory field must contain valid information.

---

### Consistency

Terminology, formatting, and category usage must remain uniform throughout the dataset.

---

### Accuracy

Knowledge must accurately represent the developer's internship observations.

---

### Traceability

Every record must be traceable to a unique identifier and source note.

---

### Repeatability

Applying the same validation procedure should always produce the same result.

---

## 3.6.4 Levels of Validation

Validation is performed at multiple levels.

### Field-Level Validation

Verifies individual fields.

Examples include:

- Missing values
- Invalid category names
- Empty answers
- Invalid identifiers

---

### Record-Level Validation

Verifies the complete record.

Checks include:

- Single-question principle
- Self-contained answer
- Correct category
- Source note availability

---

### Dataset-Level Validation

Verifies the dataset as a whole.

Checks include:

- Duplicate knowledge
- Category balance
- Identifier uniqueness
- Terminology consistency
- Dataset completeness

## 3.6.5 Mandatory Field Validation

Every record must contain the following mandatory fields.

| Field            | Validation Requirement |
| ---------------- | ---------------------- |
| Record ID        | Present and unique     |
| Category         | Valid category value   |
| Primary Question | Non-empty              |
| Answer           | Non-empty              |
| Source Note      | Non-empty              |

A record missing any mandatory field fails validation.

---

## 3.6.6 Identifier Validation

Each record identifier must satisfy the following conditions:

- Unique across the dataset.
- Correct category code.
- Proper numbering format.
- Stable throughout the record lifecycle.
- Never reused after retirement.

Identifier validation supports traceability throughout the project.

---

## 3.6.7 Category Validation

Every record must:

- Belong to exactly one category.
- Use one of the six approved categories.
- Match the category code embedded in its identifier.
- Follow the category boundaries defined in Dataset Category Design.

Incorrect category assignments require correction before approval.

---

## 3.6.8 Content Validation

Content validation evaluates the quality of questions and answers.

Questions should:

- Be natural.
- Represent one information need.
- Avoid ambiguity.
- Match the assigned category.

Answers should:

- Fully answer the question.
- Be accurate.
- Remain self-contained.
- Use consistent terminology.
- Avoid unsupported assumptions.

---

## 3.6.9 Duplicate Validation

Duplicate validation ensures that identical knowledge is not stored multiple times.

The validation process checks for:

- Duplicate questions.
- Duplicate answers.
- Duplicate facts expressed differently.
- Similar records within and across categories.

When duplicate knowledge is found:

- The existing record is retained.
- Additional phrasings are added as alternate questions where appropriate.
- Duplicate records are removed or retired.

---

## 3.6.10 Terminology Validation

Technical terminology should remain consistent throughout the dataset.

Validation verifies:

- Uniform terminology.
- Standard abbreviations.
- Consistent spelling.
- Proper terminology notes where required.

Consistent terminology improves retrieval quality and dataset readability.

---

## 3.6.11 Source Validation

Every record must include a source note indicating where the knowledge originated during the internship.

Validation checks that the source note:

- Exists.
- Is meaningful.
- Identifies the observation context.
- Supports future review.

Source notes are used internally and are not displayed to users.

---

## 3.6.12 Category Coverage Validation

The completed dataset should represent every finalized category.

Validation confirms:

- Every category contains records.
- Category distribution remains reasonably balanced.
- No category is significantly underrepresented.

This supports comprehensive evaluation of the chatbot.

---

## 3.6.13 Validation Workflow

Dataset validation follows the sequence below.

1. Verify mandatory fields.
2. Validate identifiers.
3. Validate category assignment.
4. Review question quality.
5. Review answer quality.
6. Check terminology consistency.
7. Detect duplicate knowledge.
8. Validate source notes.
9. Verify dataset-wide consistency.
10. Approve or return the record for correction.

Each validation stage must be completed before the next begins.

---

## 3.6.14 Validation Outcomes

Each record receives one of three outcomes.

### Pass

The record satisfies every validation requirement.

The record proceeds to approval.

---

### Revision Required

Minor issues are identified.

The record returns for correction before validation is repeated.

---

### Rejected

Major structural or knowledge issues prevent inclusion.

The record must be rewritten or removed.

---

## 3.6.15 Expected Outcome

Completion of the Dataset Validation Design establishes a repeatable quality assurance process for the ALIS knowledge base.

This validation framework ensures:

- Complete records.
- Correct category assignment.
- Unique identifiers.

- Reliable dataset quality.
- Strong traceability.
